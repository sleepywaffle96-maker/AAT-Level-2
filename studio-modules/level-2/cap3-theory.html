Chapter 3: Cash Book Mechanics, Petty Cash & Imprest Controls

1. The Hybrid Nature of the Cash Book

In bookkeeping architecture, the Cash Book holds a completely unique position. While day books (like the SDB and PDB) act strictly as books of prime entry to summarize transactions before ledger posting, the Cash Book operates as both a book of prime entry and a core ledger profile within the double-entry system itself.

When cash or electronic bank transactions are entered directly into the Cash Book, the first half of the double-entry loop is completed instantly. It records the chronological data stream of cash movements, and its final calculated balances down represent the actual financial current asset value displayed on the firm's balance sheet. It is divided rigidly into two operational sides:

  • The Debit Side (Receipts): Located on the left-hand column matrix. It accumulates all funds entering the business ecosystem (e.g., electronic bank transfers from credit customers, direct cash sales, or capital introduced by the owner). Any entry here completes a **debit entry**, increasing business assets.
  • The Credit Side (Payments): Located on the right-hand column matrix. It accumulates all liquid funds leaving the organization (e.g., electronic vendor settlements, wages paid, overhead cheques issued, or owner's drawings). Any entry here completes a **credit entry**, decreasing business assets.

2. The Architecture of the Three-Column Cash Book

To accurately track different liquid resource pools, professional organizations utilize a Three-Column Cash Book. Both the debit and credit sides feature three individual monetary analysis columns running parallel to each other:

  1. Cash Column (£): Tracks the physical legal tender, banknotes, and coins stored securely inside the on-premises safe or till.
  2. Bank Column (£): Tracks the electronic funds, automated BACS transfers, debit card clearing streams, and direct bank balances held in the company's commercial account.
  3. Discount Column (£): A completely separate column dedicated exclusively to tracking cash reductions arising from early settlement agreements.

The Memorandum Discount Exception: Under standard AAT examination criteria, the Discount columns embedded inside the Cash Book are strictly memorandum records and do not form part of the balancing double-entry cash matrix. Unlike the Cash and Bank columns, they are never balanced against each other. Instead, their period-end totals are posted independently into the General Ledger:

• The total of the Debit Discount column represents Discounts Allowed (an expense/financial cost to our firm) and is debited to the Discounts Allowed account.

• The total of the Credit Discount column represents Discounts Received (a financial benefit/income gain) and is credited to the Discounts Received account.

3. The Mechanics of Contra Entries

When financial resources are moved internally within the business—without entering or leaving the organization's global ecosystem—the bookkeeper executes a Contra Entry. A contra transaction occurs when cash is physically taken from the office safe and deposited into the company bank account, or when cash is withdrawn from the bank account to top up the office till.

Because both accounts (Cash and Bank) are held side-by-side within the same Cash Book register, the transaction is completed entirely on its pages without needing any external ledger postings. To preserve the audit trail, these entries are marked with a specific reference code letter 'C' in the ledger folio column, signaling that the corresponding debit or credit resides within the same book. The pathways are structured as follows:

• Depositing Cash into the Bank Account:

1. DEBIT the Bank column (increasing bank funds on the receipts side).
2. CREDIT the Cash column (decreasing physical currency on the payments side).

• Withdrawing Bank Funds to top up Cash till:

1. DEBIT the Cash column (increasing physical currency on the receipts side).
2. CREDIT the Bank column (decreasing bank funds on the payments side).

4. Petty Cash Architecture & The Imprest Control Mechanism

For minor, everyday administrative office expenditures (such as milk, tea, postage stamps, emergency stationery, or train tickets), writing electronic commercial bank payments or using corporate debit cards for small values is inefficient. To resolve this, bookkeeping infrastructure utilizes a separate physical cash fund kept on the premises called the Petty Cash Box.

To prevent fraud and maintain strict financial control, this fund is managed under a mathematical protocol known as the Imprest System. Under an imprest framework, the petty cash fund is allocated a fixed, legally defined baseline cash pool known as the Imprest Float (e.g., £200.00). The framework operates in a continuous operational loop:

  1. The petty cashier starts the trading period with the full, physical Imprest Float cash pool.
  2. During the period, disbursements are paid out of the box, and every single expenditure must be backed by an authorized, signed Petty Cash Voucher (PCV) listing net costs and VAT.
  3. At any given milestone, the total value of the physical cash remaining in the box plus the total monetary value of all signed vouchers inside the box must exactly equate to the initial fixed float level.
  4. At the end of the window, the main cashier checks the vouchers and issues a top-up reimbursement cheque from the company bank account for an amount exactly equal to the total value of the vouchers spent, systematically restoring the fund back to its float baseline.

5. Comprehensive Worked Example: Imprest Restoration and VAT Extraction

Let us review an academic case study to observe the calculations. Vanguard Retail maintains a strict petty cash imprest float set at £150.00. During the week, the petty cashier pays out for the following three authorized vouchers:

  • Voucher 1: Office Milk and Tea supplies — £12.00 Gross (Subject to standard 20% VAT).
  • Voucher 2: Emergency Postage Stamps — £45.00 Gross (Exempt from VAT).
  • Voucher 3: Train ticket for client meeting — £24.00 Gross (Zero-Rated 0% VAT).

Step 1: Extract VAT and Net values for Voucher 1 (Standard 20% VAT)
Using the standard VAT fraction: £12.00 ÷ 6 = £2.00 (Input VAT component).
Net Expense value: £12.00 - £2.00 = £10.00 (Net Sundry Office Expense).

Step 2: Aggregate Total Petty Cash Expenditures
Total Cash Spent = Voucher 1 (£12) + Voucher 2 (£45) + Voucher 3 (£24) = £81.00.

Step 3: Determine Expected Cash Remaining in Box
Initial Float Baseline (£150.00) - Total Cash Spent (£81.00) = £69.00 (Expected Cash in Hand).

Step 4: Calculate the Restoration Reimbursement Required
To restore the box from its current cash position (£69.00) back to the float level (£150.00), the main cashier must issue a bank reimbursement transfer for an amount exactly equal to the total expenditures: £81.00.

6. Investigating Cash Discrepancies (Shortages & Surpluses)

At the end of a monitoring window, before the main cashier issues the top-up reimbursement transfer, a physical hand count of the remaining cash banknotes and coins must be executed. This physical count is cross-checked against the mathematical paperwork. If the physical cash count does not match the expected cash balance, a discrepancy exists:

  • Cash Shortage: Occurs when the actual physical cash in the box is less than the expected balance calculated from the vouchers. This represents a financial loss to the business (typically caused by giving incorrect change or losing coins) and must be recorded as an overhead expense.
  • Cash Surplus: Occurs when the actual physical cash in the box is greater than the expected balance. This represents an auxiliary accounting gain and is treated as credit income.

The Accounting Correction Matrix: If a cash discrepancy cannot be resolved immediately, the difference cannot be ignored. The bookkeeper must record the shortage or surplus value inside a temporary general ledger account called the Suspense Account / Cash Discrepancy Account to balance the books while internal investigations take place.

7. Double-Entry General Ledger Postings for Petty Cash

When the petty cash book columns are summarized and the reimbursement transfer is executed, the double-entry accounting loop must be completed within the **General Ledger**. The postings are divided into two distinct operational steps:

Step 1: Recording the Month-End Analysis Expenses (The Post)

The total net expenditures from the analysis columns and the total VAT column are extracted from the petty cash book and posted to the General Ledger as follows:

  • DEBIT the individual Nominal Overhead Accounts (e.g., Office Stationery, Travel Expenses, Cleaning Expenses) with their respective Net column totals.
  • DEBIT the VAT Control Account with the cumulative VAT column total (reclaimable Input VAT asset).
  • CREDIT the Petty Cash Control Account with the global **Gross total of all vouchers spent**.

Step 2: Recording the Bank Reimbursement (The Top-Up)

When the main bank account issues the top-up check to restore the physical cash pool inside the box, the following entry is executed:

  • DEBIT the Petty Cash Control Account (increasing the asset value of the petty cash box).
  • CREDIT the primary Bank Account (decreasing the main cash assets inside the bank column).

8. Summary of Cash Controls Hierarchy

Maintaining clear boundaries between different ledger cash streams is vital for corporate transparency. The Cash Book monitors primary liquid banking and business-wide cash streams, while the Petty Cash ledger acts as a dedicated micro-controlled ecosystem. Both networks require continuous validation through regular cash counts and bank reconciliations to ensure internal audit security and error minimization.
