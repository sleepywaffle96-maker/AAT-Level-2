Chapter 2: Books of Prime Entry, Invoicing Mechanics & VAT

1. The Architecture of Prime Entry Books

In a high-volume corporate environment, posting every single individual credit invoice directly into the core General Ledger accounts would immediately clutter the financial records and increase the risk of operational errors. To prevent this, bookkeeping architecture utilizes specialized sub-journals known as Books of Prime Entry (or Day Books).

These books act as a primary staging area. Their function is to log, classify, and summarize transactions of the exact same operational nature chronologically before any double-entry bookkeeping takes place. The four core day books required for credit transactions are:

  • Sales Day Book (SDB): A chronological summary list of all credit sales invoices issued to customers.
  • Sales Returns Day Book (SRDB): Logs all credit notes issued to credit customers for returned faulty stock or overcharge corrections.
  • Purchase Day Book (PDB): A chronological list summarizing all credit purchase invoices received from trade suppliers.
  • Purchase Returns Day Book (PRDB): Logs all credit notes received from trade suppliers for stock returned by our firm.

2. Value Added Tax (VAT) Regulations & Flow Frameworks

Value Added Tax (VAT) is an indirect consumption tax charged on the supply of goods and services within the UK, administered strictly by HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC). A business whose taxable turnover exceeds the statutory registration threshold must register for VAT and systematically track its tax flows:

  • Output VAT (Liability): Charged by our business on credit and cash sales to customers. This represents money collected on behalf of the government and is a Current Liability owed to HMRC.
  • Input VAT (Asset): Suffered by our business on purchases and operational overhead overheads from other VAT-registered firms. This represents tax that can be legally recovered from the government and acts as a Current Asset.

Tax Classifications Checklist:

• Standard Rate (20%): Applies to the vast majority of commercial goods and trading activities.

• Zero Rate (0%): Taxable at 0% (e.g., books, children's clothes, basic food staples). Input VAT on associated purchases can still be fully reclaimed.

• Exempt (No VAT): Completely outside the VAT network (e.g., insurance, postage stamps, bank financial fees). No VAT is charged, and Input VAT on associated costs cannot be reclaimed.

3. The Invoicing Pipeline & Trade Discounts

When preparing or checking a trade invoice, numbers must be processed in a rigid mathematical sequence. A common area of confusion is the application of a Trade Discount. A trade discount is a reduction in the catalog list price granted to other businesses operating in the same industry or for high-volume orders.

Under AAT guidelines, trade discounts must be deducted before VAT is calculated. The net amount on the invoice represents the price after the trade discount, and it is this net amount that forms the tax base for the 20% standard rate. The calculation sequence is structured as follows:

  1. Gross List Price (Catalog baseline)
  2. Less: Trade Discount (Calculated as a % of List Price)
  3. = Net Invoice Value (The absolute tax base for VAT)
  4. Add: VAT Component (Net Invoice Value × 20%)
  5. = Gross Invoice Total (The total monetary amount due from the customer)

4. Comprehensive Worked Example: Invoice Calculation

Let us analyze a real-world scenario to observe the mathematical flow. Vanguard Retail purchases a batch of 100 inventory units from Alpha Wholesalers. The catalog list price is £10 per unit (£1,000 total). Alpha Wholesalers grants a 15% trade volume discount. The transaction is subject to standard VAT at 20%.

Step 1: Calculate Gross List Price Base
100 units × £10 = £1,000.00

Step 2: Deduct Trade Discount (15%)
£1,000.00 × 15% = £150.00 discount value.
£1,000.00 - £150.00 = £850.00 (Net Invoice Value)

Step 3: Calculate VAT Component (20% on Net)
£850.00 × 20% = £170.00 (VAT Amount)

Step 4: Compute Gross Total Due
£850.00 (Net) + £170.00 (VAT) = £1,020.00 (Gross Invoice Total)

Critical Check: The buyer owes the supplier £1,020.00. The cost to the business is only the net amount of £850.00, while the £170.00 represents Input VAT that will be reclaimed from HMRC.

5. Day Book Logging Architecture

Once the components of the source invoice are verified, the clerk enters the details into the appropriate Book of Prime Entry. Every credit day book features a minimum of three distinct monetary analysis columns designed to keep values separate: Net, VAT, and Gross.

Below is how the credit transaction from our worked example is systematically logged inside Alpha Wholesalers' Sales Day Book (SDB):

Date Invoice No. Customer Account Name Net Sales Column (£) VAT Output Column (£) Gross Total Column (£)
12/06/2026 INV-8801 Vanguard Retail 850.00 170.00 1,020.00
PERIODIC AGGREGATED TOTALS 850.00 170.00 1,020.00

6. The Mechanics of VAT Fraction Extraction (Vat Inclusive)

In many bookkeeping assessments, you will be faced with a gross inclusive total and required to extract the hidden net and tax components. When dealing with standard 20% VAT, the quickest way to extract the VAT component from a gross total is to use the VAT Fraction:

VAT Component = Gross Inclusive Total ÷ 6

Alternatively, you can divide the gross total by 1.20 to isolate the net amount. Let us review an example: A business receives an invoice for utility services with a gross inclusive total of £720.

  • Isolate VAT Amount: £720 ÷ 6 = £120.00
  • Isolate Net Expense Value: £720 ÷ 1.20 = £600.00 (or £720 - £120 VAT)

7. Double-Entry Posting Protocols from Day Books

At regular, defined intervals (usually weekly or monthly), the columns of the Day Books are totaled, and these aggregated figures are used to execute double-entry postings within the **General Ledger**. The transfer path is highly structured and must follow these exact protocols:

A. Posting the Sales Day Book (SDB) Totals:

The cumulative totals are posted to the General Ledger as follows:

  • DEBIT the Sales Ledger Control Account (SLCA) with the Gross Total. This records the total asset value owed by credit customers.
  • CREDIT the Sales Revenue Account with the Net Total. This records the true income earned by the business.
  • CREDIT the VAT Control Account with the VAT Total. This logs the Output VAT liability due to HMRC.

B. Posting the Purchase Day Book (PDB) Totals:

The cumulative totals are posted to the General Ledger as follows:

  • DEBIT the Purchases Account with the Net Total. This records the core operational stock expense value.
  • DEBIT the VAT Control Account with the VAT Total. This logs the recoverable Input VAT asset.
  • CREDIT the Purchase Ledger Control Account (PLCA) with the Gross Total. This records the total liability owed to trade creditors.

8. Subsidiary Ledgers & Memorandum Posting Flows

While the General Ledger handles the aggregate double-entry control totals, a business must also keep track of exactly how much money is owed by each individual customer or owed to each individual supplier. This detailed tracking occurs inside the **Subsidiary Ledgers**:

  • Sales Ledger (SL): Contains the individual personal accounts of each credit customer.
  • Purchase Ledger (PL): Contains the individual personal accounts of each credit supplier.

The Memorandum Rule: Individual invoices are posted into these personal accounts day-by-day, directly from the rows of the day books. However, these subsidiary ledgers act purely as memorandum records. They are outside the core double-entry matrix and are used solely for credit control and tracking individual statement balances.
