Drafting & Interpreting Financial Statements (DAIF): Financial Analysis & Ratios

AAT Level 4 Syllabus Context: This unit evaluates corporate performance through advanced accounting ratios, investor performance analysis, liquidity health checks, and working capital cycles.

1. Profitability and Return Metrics

Interpreting corporate financial statements requires standardising figures into ratios to compare performance over time or across competitors within the same market sector.

Return on Capital Employed (ROCE)

ROCE is the primary measure of operational profitability. It evaluates how efficiently management utilizes the total long-term capital invested in the enterprise (Equity plus Non-Current Liabilities) to generate a trading return. It isolates financial performance from tax environments and leverage choices by using **Operating Profit** (Profit Before Interest and Tax - PBIT).

Asset Turnover

Measures corporate efficiency in utilizing assets to generate sales revenue. A low asset turnover indicates that the business is over-capitalised or has non-productive resources, while a high ratio often correlates with intense capacity utilization or lean operations.

2. Liquidity, Solvency, and Working Capital Risk

A business can be highly profitable but still collapse if it runs out of cash to clear short-term liabilities on time.

Liquidity Indicators

  • Current Ratio: Measures total current assets against current liabilities. A standard benchmark is 1.5 to 2.0, though this varies by industry.
  • Acid Test Ratio (Quick Ratio): Excludes inventory from current assets because inventory is the least liquid short-term resource and cannot be converted into immediate cash during an emergency financial crisis.

The Working Capital Operating Cycle

Measures the speed at which raw cash is converted back into cash through standard trading processes. It aggregates three operational dimensions:

  • Inventory Holding Period: The average number of days items remain locked in stock warehouses before being sold.
  • Trade Receivables Collection Period: The average time credit clients take to clear outstanding invoices.
  • Trade Payables Payment Period: The average timeframe the business takes to settle invoices with its trade suppliers.

3. Gearing and Financial Risk Paradigms

Gearing evaluates the capital structure of a corporate entity by calculating the proportion of long-term funding provided by debt (e.g., debentures, bank loans) versus equity (shareholders' funds).

High gearing indicates increased financial risk because debt requires compulsory interest payments regardless of trading profitability, leaving the firm vulnerable to insolvency during economic recessions. Conversely, low gearing provides safety but may restrict growth capital.
