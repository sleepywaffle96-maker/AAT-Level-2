Financial Accounting: Preparing Financial Statements (FAPS)

AAT Syllabus Context: This chapter covers the conceptual framework, accounting principles, and the generation of final accounts for sole traders and partnerships from incomplete records and trial balances.

1. The Conceptual Framework and Accounting Principles

The preparation of financial statements is governed by international accounting standards adopted in the UK (IFRS/UK GAAP). Financial reporting must provide high-quality, transparent, and comparable information to stakeholders such as lenders, investors, and HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC).

Qualitative Characteristics

Under the IASB Conceptual Framework, financial information must possess fundamental and enhancing qualitative characteristics:

  • Relevance: Information must be capable of making a difference in the decisions made by users. It has predictive or confirmatory value.
  • Faithful Representation: The information must faithfully represent the economic phenomena it purports to represent. It must be complete, neutral, and free from error.
  • Comparability: Enables users to identify and understand similarities in, and differences among, items across periods or entities.
  • Verifiability: Assures users that information faithfully represents the economic phenomena it purports to represent.
  • Timeliness: Having information available to decision-makers in time to be capable of influencing their decisions.
  • Understandability: Classifying, characterising, and presenting information clearly and concisely makes it understandable.

2. Capital and Revenue Expenditure

Distinguishing correctly between capital and revenue items is vital to avoid material misstatements in the Statement of Profit or Loss (SoPL) and the Statement of Financial Position (SoFP).

Capital Expenditure results in the acquisition or improvement of non-current assets. This includes the purchase price, legal fees, delivery costs, and installation costs. It is capitalised on the SoFP.

Revenue Expenditure relates to the day-to-day running costs of the business, such as repairs, maintenance, insurance, and wages. These items are expensed directly to the SoPL.

3. Adjustments for Financial Statements

Before final financial statements can be produced from a Trial Balance, several year-end adjustments must be processed via ledger entries:

Accruals and Prepayments

Based on the accruals concept (matching principle), income and expenses must be recognised in the period they occur, not when cash changes hands.

  • Accrued Expenses: Expenses incurred but not yet invoiced or paid at year-end. (Liability on SoFP).
  • Prepaid Expenses: Expenses paid in advance for the next accounting period. (Current Asset on SoFP).

Irrecoverable Debts and Allowance for Doubtful Debts

When a customer is definitely unable to pay, the balance must be written off as an irrecoverable debt expense in the SoPL. If there is uncertainty regarding future collections, an allowance for doubtful debts is created or adjusted based on the prudence concept, reducing trade receivables on the SoFP.

4. Partnership Accounting

Partnerships require distinct accounting structures to allocate profits among owners according to a Partnership Agreement. If no agreement exists, the Partnership Act 1890 applies (equal profit split, no interest on capital).

Profits are allocated using a Partnership Appropriation Account after calculating the net trading profit. Adjustments include:

  • Salaries to Partners: Deducted from total profit before general distribution.
  • Interest on Capital: Rewards partners for their capital investment.
  • Interest on Drawings: Penalises excessive withdrawals from the firm.

Each partner has a Capital Account (fixed investment) and a Current Account (day-to-day transactions, drawings, and share of profits).
