Business Awareness: Internal Controls & Ethics

AAT Syllabus Context: This chapter evaluates the strength of accounting systems, the identification of corporate fraud, data protection laws, and the mandatory code of professional ethics.

1. The Internal Control Environment

Internal controls are the policies and processes implemented by management to safeguard corporate assets, ensure financial accuracy, and prevent operational fraud.

The Five Components of Internal Control

  • Control Environment: The overall tone set by management regarding the importance of ethics and compliance.
  • Risk Assessment: Identifying and analysing operational risks that could prevent the business from achieving its goals.
  • Control Activities: Policies such as physical security, authorisations, and segregation of duties.
  • Information and Communication: Ensuring relevant financial logs are captured accurately and shared appropriately.
  • Monitoring: Continual evaluation of control systems over time to repair deficiencies.

Segregation of Duties

A core internal control mechanism where no single employee is given absolute power to handle a financial transaction from start to finish. For example, the employee who authorises a supplier payment must not be the same employee who reconciles the bank statement.

2. The AAT Code of Professional Ethics

Professional accountants must adhere to strict ethical standards. The AAT code is a principles-based framework rather than a rigid list of rules, requiring accountants to exercise professional judgment across five fundamental concepts:

  • Integrity: Being straightforward, honest, and truthful in all professional and business relationships.
  • Objectivity: Not allowing bias, conflict of interest, or undue influence of others to override professional judgment.
  • Professional Competence and Due Care: Maintaining professional knowledge and skill at the level required to ensure clients receive competent service based on current legislation.
  • Confidentiality: Respecting the confidentiality of information acquired during professional duties. Information must not be disclosed without proper authority unless there is a legal obligation to do so.
  • Professional Behaviour: Complying with relevant laws and regulations and avoiding any conduct that discredits the profession.

3. Statutory Regulations and Legal Frameworks

Accounting professionals operate within comprehensive statutory boundaries in the UK to combat financial crime and protect corporate assets:

  • Money Laundering Regulations (MLR): Accountants must report any suspicious transactions to the National Crime Agency (NCA) via a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR). Tipping off a client that they are under investigation is a criminal offence.
  • Bribery Act 2010: Criminalises giving or receiving bribes. Commercial organisations are liable if they fail to prevent bribery within their infrastructure.
  • Data Protection Act 2018 (GDPR): Governs the handling and processing of personal accounting information. Financial data must be stored securely and processed lawfully.
